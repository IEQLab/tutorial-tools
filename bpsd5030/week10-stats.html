Paired-Data Analysis Helper

Your Assignment 2 analysis companion. Paste your two conditions (one participant per row) and this describes the data, runs one appropriate test, shows the working, and models how to report it in IMRaD prose. It follows the four steps of the Week 10 analysis workshop end to end. It does the arithmetic; the words, the figure choices, and the judgement of whether the test suits your design stay yours.

Step 1. Name your design first

Before you paste anything, write down what you are comparing. This decides which test is correct, so it comes before the numbers.

  • Research question — the one difference you are testing (e.g. "Does background noise lower math-task scores at a fixed temperature?").
  • Independent variable — what you changed (e.g. noise: quiet vs noisy). Anything held constant is not a variable.
  • Dependent variable — what you measured (e.g. problems answered correctly).
  • Design — paired or independent? See the flag below; it is the most common mistake in this assignment.
This tool is for paired (within-subjects) data only: one row = one person measured in both conditions (e.g. the same participant tested quiet and noisy). If your A2 angle compares different people — for example noisy scores split by gender or by age group — that is an independent-samples comparison and needs a different test (an independent-samples t-test or Mann–Whitney). Pasting unpaired groups here will produce a number, but the wrong one. Match the test to your design.
Paired vs independent, in one line

Paired: every row is one person, and the two columns are the same people under two conditions, so you analyse each person's A − B difference. Independent: the two groups are different people, so there are no per-row differences to take and you compare the two group means directly. Counterbalancing condition order (some people quiet-first, some noisy-first) keeps a paired design valid; it does not make two separate groups paired.

Step 2. Your data

Two columns, one row per participant (paired / within-subjects). Separate values with a space, tab, or comma. A text header row is ignored.

Step 3. Describe before you test

The maths behind the mean and SD

The mean is the typical score: add the values and divide by n. The standard deviation is how far scores sit from that mean on average: SD = sqrt( sum of (value − mean)² / (n − 1) ). The n − 1 (not n) is the sample correction. Report the mean and the SD for each condition, never the mean alone: a 3-point gap means little until you know whether scores already vary by 1 point or by 10.

Step 4. Test one difference

A paired-samples t-test is the default for paired numeric data. If your data are ordinal or clearly skewed (e.g. Likert comfort ratings), use the Wilcoxon signed-rank result instead.

The maths behind the paired t-test (on your numbers)
Prefer a spreadsheet, R, Python, or AI?

Any of these gives the same paired result, so use whichever you know:

Excel: =T.TEST(A_range, B_range, 2, 1) returns the two-tailed paired p-value (the 2 = two tails, the 1 = paired). The Data Analysis add-in's "t-Test: Paired Two Sample for Means" gives the full table.

R: t.test(A, B, paired = TRUE)  ·  ordinal/skewed: wilcox.test(A, B, paired = TRUE)

Python: scipy.stats.ttest_rel(A, B)  ·  ordinal/skewed: scipy.stats.wilcoxon(A, B)

AI-assisted: you can ask an AI tool to run it, but then check the output yourself: is it a paired test, are the columns in the right order, is the p-value in a plausible range? Under the unit's Lane 2 AI stance any tool can run the test, but you own the result, not the tool.

Write the Methods too, as replicable prose

The tool cannot write your Methods, because they describe your experiment. Here is the shape a High Distinction Methods paragraph takes, on the worked-example scenario. Notice it lets another researcher reproduce the study.

Eight participants each completed a 4-minute arithmetic task twice at a fixed air temperature of 22°C, once under a quiet background (~30 dBA) and once under a noisy background (~54 dBA), with condition order counterbalanced. The score was the number of problems answered correctly. Because each participant completed both conditions, differences between conditions were assessed with a paired-samples t-test (α = 0.05). Analysis was carried out in [tool], and outputs were checked by hand against the condition means.

The same facts as an AI-style bullet list are not what to submit:

  • 8 participants
  • Math task, 4 min
  • Two conditions: quiet, noisy
  • Temperature: 22°C
  • Paired t-test used
  • Significance level 0.05

Both hold the same facts. The bullets list ingredients; the prose lets someone reproduce your study, which is the whole point of a Methods section. It states the order was counterbalanced, defines the score, justifies why a paired test (each participant did both conditions), and names the tool. A2's Methods criterion rewards the prose version.

Before you submit A2

  • Research question and IV/DV stated clearly, and the right variable labelled as which.
  • Means and SDs (or equivalent spread) reported for each condition, not means alone.
  • At least one figure with labelled axes, units, a legend where needed, and a caption that says what to notice.
  • One appropriate inferential test, correctly matched to your design (paired t-test or Wilcoxon for paired data; an independent-samples test if you compared different people).
  • The size of the effect reported alongside the p-value, in plain units.
  • Methods written as replicable prose, not a bullet list.
  • If you used AI at any step, acknowledged it (see the A2 brief) and validated its output yourself.
Paired t-test p-value from Student's t distribution (regularised incomplete beta). Wilcoxon p-value from the normal approximation with continuity and tie corrections (for small samples, confirm against exact tables or software). Effect size is Cohen's dz = mean difference / SD of differences. Teaching aid for BPSD5030; validate against software before relying on it.
